Design brief · Section 03

Meet your guide.

This is not an about section. It is a campfire introduction. The reader has just walked through the myth and the manifesto. They have seen the testimonials. Now they ask the only question that actually matters. Who is this man, and why should I keep reading. The design has to translate a person, not a product.

Functional

Establish authority without resume.

Yanik is not a guru. He is a 30 year operator who draws suns. The section has to convey seven companies, a thousand members, and a clear point of view, without looking like a LinkedIn profile.

Emotional

Invite, do not sell.

The reader should feel like they have been handed a private notebook, not a pitch. Warmth over polish. Texture over gloss.

State of mind

Skeptical but curious.

They liked the myth. They are asking, is this guy real, or is he performing real. Design has to deliver evidence, in his hand, unedited.

Brand tether

Paper, ink, sun gold.

The rest of the site lives in the journal metaphor. This section has to honor that language while standing as its own moment, not a repeat.

Conversion physics

Polarize cleanly.

The cosmic sloth framing is either a yes or a no. We do not hedge. We pick a register and commit. Skeptics self select out, the right reader leans in.

Three directions

One brief. Three honest takes.

Each option below is a real, fully formed answer to the brief. They differ in visual metaphor, typographic register, and how much of Yanik's body and handwriting the reader sees. Pick the one that feels most like him to you, and we build it.

Design direction

Full bleed spread of a warm cream paper background with two faintly ruled pages and a gentle center gutter. The left page is the evidence page. A black and white portrait printed as a polaroid, paperclipped, with a handwritten caption. Below it, a second polaroid showing one of his sun doodles. A faded Maverick1000 ticket stub in the bottom corner with a real ink handstamp.

The right page is his voice. A handwritten title in burnt orange ink. Below it, typed paragraphs in Fraunces that read like a letter, not a bio. Key phrases ("cage made out of money", "thousand entrepreneurs") are italicized as if he underlined them. His signature lives at the bottom in Caveat.

Stage transitions are tactile. Scroll past the spread and a subtle paper curl animation reveals the next page. The whole section is one continuous object, not a grid of cards.

Strategic rationale

This is the most on brand, most defensible choice. It is what the reader already believes the site promised them from the hero and the myth prologue. The journal metaphor is not new here, it is kept. That continuity is the point.

Design psychology: the reader is in a skeptical phase by the time they reach this section. A bio page is where most personal brands break trust by puffing themselves up. The journal spread does the opposite. It makes the reader feel like they are spying on something honest, which paradoxically makes them trust the author more.

Risk to manage. Fidelity trap. If the polaroid edges, paper texture, or ticket stamp feel themed rather than scanned, the whole thing collapses into kitsch. We commission real scans of Yanik's own journal and one real Maverick ticket. No Photoshop texture presets.

Why this scores 89

It is the highest fidelity translation of the brief. It continues the mythology, uses real assets we already have, and gives the voice section breathing room. It scores 89 and not 95 because it is also the most predictable move, which means it leans on execution quality more than invention. If the paper textures are a hair off, the magic dies.

Design direction

A single full width cinema plate. The left 55% is a macro photograph of Yanik's cosmic sloth tattoo, filling the frame. Low contrast, warm skin tones, deep black backdrop. The tattoo is the hero, not a thumbnail.

The right 45% sits in a matte black field. A tiny gold eyebrow. A three line declarative headline set in Fraunces display. One italic word, "cosmic sloth", pulls the eye. A short lede. A signature in Caveat gold, as if he just closed a letter. Three raw stats at the bottom, no chrome, no labels. Seven. One thousand. Infinity.

Handwritten annotations float over the tattoo in gold ink, pointing at details the way a museum label points at a brushstroke. "The sloth, inked 2023." "A thousand suns, one quiet body."

Strategic rationale

This is the most memorable option. It makes his body the proof, not his resume. In his own words, he has the brand tattooed on him, and the site would be wasting time not to lead with it. The tattoo is the single most polarizing, self authenticating asset we have. Every doubt about whether he is performing the cosmic sloth story collapses the moment you see it inked on his skin.

Design psychology: skin and ink make a biological claim that copy cannot. When a reader sees a permanent mark on a real body, their brain registers commitment at a different level than reading a bio. This is the same reason tattoo culture shots convert better than stock portraits in editorial branding.

Risk to manage. It is polarizing by design. Some readers will bounce on sight. That is fine, and arguably correct for the brand, but we should ship it knowing the opportunity cost is real.

Why this scores 83

Highest ceiling, narrower base. If the right reader lands here, they are sold in four seconds. If the wrong reader lands here, they bounce. I score it 83 because its power is also its cost. It is also slightly less rich than Option 1 in terms of giving the reader content to hold onto. We lose the polaroid stack, the ticket stub, the multiple artifacts. We gain one cinema moment in exchange.

Section 03 · The Guide

The man who drew himself out of a cage made of money.

They call him the cosmic sloth, but only because he finally stopped fighting it. Yanik Silver sold his first internet company at twenty nine and discovered he had built, in his own words, a cage made out of money.

So he tore it down. He started drawing suns. A thousand so far, and counting. Around those suns he gathered a thousand entrepreneurs who had all hit the same wall he had. They call themselves Mavericks, because the world kept calling them that anyway.

He runs seven companies and a monthly community. He journals every morning. He has the brand tattooed on his own body.

Design direction

A cinematic landscape portrait fills the top half, cropped wide, with soft vignette pulling the eye center. A tight gold eyebrow locates the reader. A display headline in third person pulls a line from the actual transcript, treated like a magazine cover. Italic word, "drew himself", does the work.

The bottom half is an editorial two column. Left column is the letter, set long form in Fraunces with a drop cap in sun gold. Right column is a pull quote in Caveat gold, followed by a stat panel with tight facts. No buttons, no CTAs. The section ends when the story does.

Sparseness is the signature. No polaroids, no tape, no stamps. The design leans on photography and typography alone. It is the version the reader can show to their more conservative friend without having to defend the aesthetic.

Strategic rationale

This is the option a Harvard MBA, a Joe Polish introduction, or a private equity operator forwards to a friend. It reads as a serious magazine profile, which is the correct register if we are trying to attract the 1000 entrepreneurs Yanik actually wants, not just the aesthetic tribe.

Design psychology: third person framing signals pedigree. When Yanik describes himself, it reads as performance. When a designer frames him in third person, it reads as editorial coverage. We reclaim a pound of authority without losing the voice, because the letter column is still his words.

Risk to manage. It is the least tactile option. Readers arriving from the journal prologue may feel the aesthetic break. We can mitigate by keeping paper cream as the cream note in the pull column and by closing with a handwritten signature, so the reader lands back in the journal before the next section.

Why this scores 79

Strong, safe, and legible. Scores 79 because it is the most conservative take on the brief. It is also the least differentiated from other founder sites the reader has seen this week. If our audience is already bought into the mythology, this option is slightly too buttoned up. If our audience is a first pass skeptic, it is the option that passes their smell test most easily.

How to choose

Which register is Yanik in today.

The three options each solve the brief. They are not equivalent. They differ in what they ask of the reader, what they trade for attention, and how far into the mythology they lean. Use the matrix to decide.

My recommendation

Ship Option 01, The Open Journal, as the main section. It is the highest fidelity, most defensible, and most on brand answer to the brief. Then steal one move from Option 02: use the tattoo macro photograph as the last panel of the spread, so the journal metaphor gives way to the body metaphor exactly where the reader is deepest into Yanik's voice. That combination solves the "polarize fast" ask without losing the tactile journal integrity. Confidence in the combined approach, 92.
