Meet your guide.

Three fresh takes, nothing from the first round carried over. No journal spread, no tattoo macro, no magazine profile. Copy is rewritten without the old money origin story. This section introduces a sun one, a cosmic sloth, a man who runs a thousand entrepreneurs and journals every morning. Not a founder exit story.

What changed in this round

New photos. New voice. New shapes.

The first round leaned on two portraits we already had and an origin story that reads as a founder exit. Yanik does not actually live in that story. He lives in the drawing, the sun, the community, the morning journal, the cacao, the sloth tattoo on his skin. These three options are built around that version of him. Photos now include the kangaroo moment, the mustache stage shot, the sloth onesie with family, and the Branson interview. Copy draws from his own words in the transcript, not a fictionalized exit.

Option 01 · Letter from ten years ahead

The handoff.

One handwritten letter on cream paper, from Yanik to the reader. Not an introduction. A handoff from someone who has walked ten more years of the same path and is quietly leaving you a map.

Design direction

Full bleed cream paper fills the whole section, with soft ruled lines faintly showing through. A paper clipped snapshot sits in the top right, at a gentle 3 degree tilt, showing Yanik relaxed and natural with an animal. Below it, a handwritten greeting in burnt orange ink. The body of the letter is set in Fraunces, 17px, long form, like a magazine letter.

One highlighted line does the heavy lifting. "The sun is the only honest teacher I have found." Highlighted in a translucent sun gold wash so it reads as if Yanik had run a highlighter over it while drafting.

Signature in Caveat, the same hand as the greeting. A post script at the bottom, set off by a hairline dashed rule, hints at the mythology without explaining it. A single marginal annotation in burnt orange points at the best line.

Visual spec
Type
Fraunces body 17px · Caveat 20 to 40px · Space Grotesk 10px for stamps
Palette
Paper cream, paper shadow, burnt orange ink, sun gold highlight, ink.
Layout
Full bleed paper. Inner margin 76px desktop, 32px mobile. Max line length 58 characters.
Motion
Letter fades in line by line on scroll. Highlight underline strokes in left to right. Signature ink bleeds on last reveal.
Strategic rationale

The letter inverts the bio problem. A bio says, "here is who I am, please care." A letter says, "I already know who you are, and I wrote this for you." The reader is not asked to evaluate Yanik. They are invited into a private message. That shift changes the entire emotional register of the section.

Design psychology: letters carry an intimacy that web copy cannot manufacture. Pen ink, paper, greeting, signature, post script. Every element signals a private, personal, uncopyable act. The reader reads more slowly, trusts more deeply, and remembers longer.

Why this scores 89. Highest emotional resonance on the brief. Most on brand continuity with the myth prologue and the journal aesthetic without literally repeating the journal spread pattern. Loses a couple of points to execution risk: if the handwriting is a canned Caveat string and not actually his, the letter collapses into theme. We commission two or three key phrases scanned from Yanik's real hand.

What this asks the reader

Read me. I wrote this for you. If you hear yourself in it, the rest of the site is for you. If you do not, close the tab and go back to whatever you were doing. No offense taken.

Design direction

Two columns of equal width. Dark, warm, lamplight tones. Left column speaks to the reader in second person, "You built the thing. You got quieter once you did." Right column speaks in Yanik's first person, paced so each line rhymes with the line opposite it.

A single circular portrait sits at the exact pivot of the two columns, centered on the vertical divider line. Thin gold rule glows faintly between the columns. The portrait anchors the gaze and makes the reader look at Yanik only after they have read themselves in the left column first.

Both columns close with a signature line. Left: a gentle "that itch is why you are on this page." Right: "yours, still drawing, Yanik." Signatures meet at the middle. The reader has been introduced to themselves and to him in the same beat.

Visual spec
Type
Fraunces display 30 to 42px · Fraunces body 18px · Caveat 26px signature right, Fraunces italic 17px left
Palette
Ink dark, warmer ink dark, sun gold, gold bright, paper highlight.
Layout
50/50 two column. Center circular portrait 124px diameter. Vertical divider 1px gold gradient. Mobile stacks, portrait sits between stacks as a horizontal rule.
Motion
Left column reveals on scroll. Right column reveals the same lines, delayed 200ms, so the eye catches the rhyme live. Portrait fades in on both columns complete.
Strategic rationale

This is the highest conversion play on the page. Readers do not buy from people whose story impresses them. They buy from people whose story recognizes them. The mirror structure literally forces recognition first and introduction second. By the time they read his name, they have already nodded along three times.

Design psychology: the mirror is the single most effective sales pattern in long form copywriting. Gary Halbert's hospital letter, every Basecamp landing page, Morning Brew's founder story. The reason they work is that second person narration turns the page into a diagnostic instrument. The reader reads themselves diagnosed, then reads the person who understands the diagnosis.

Why this scores 92. This is the strongest option. It hits recognition, it hits brand voice, it lets a single well chosen photograph do the heavy lifting, and it sets up the rest of the site to feel like a journey the reader is already on, not a pitch they are considering. It loses a few points because rhyming two columns well is a copy craft problem as much as a design problem. The design holds up even if the copy is average, but the magic only lands if both halves are written in parallel with care.

What this asks the reader

Look to the left. Then look to the right. If the two sides are describing the same person, welcome home.

Design direction

Dark radial background that reads like a desk lit by a single warm lamp. Nine handwritten index cards are scattered across a twelve column grid, each with a slight rotation. Cards alternate between text only and small photographs.

Text cards carry one sentence per card in Caveat, with selective emphasis: brand words in burnt orange, italics in ink blue, key phrases with a hand drawn underline. Photo cards use real images with a small handwritten caption strip ("exhibit A. the onesie.") at the bottom.

Numbering is monospace stamped in small sepia type. One card per beat. The reader does not read a bio, they watch Yanik place evidence on the table, one card at a time.

Visual spec
Type
Caveat 20 to 24px primary · Space Grotesk 9px card numbers · Fraunces 28px section header
Palette
Dark oak, near black, card cream, burnt orange, ink blue.
Layout
12 column grid, 14px gap. Cards span 3 to 8 columns. Rotations ranged minus 2 to plus 2.2 degrees.
Motion
Cards drop in sequence with a subtle bounce. Shadows grow on hover. Photo cards tilt 1 degree on hover for a micro lift.
Strategic rationale

The index card form is the most native to how Yanik actually thinks. He journals. He doodles. He notes. A bio expects to be read linearly. An index card scatter says, read whichever line you want first. That matches the reader who is skimming, and it rewards the reader who stays.

Design psychology: fragmentation reads as honesty. The brain processes a complete paragraph as rhetoric, while a set of discrete one liners reads as evidence. Each card becomes a micro commitment. The photograph cards land as proof exhibits in a case he is making about himself.

Why this scores 82. Native to his voice, high texture, most shareable by screenshot. Loses a few points because it is visually busier than the other two, which means if the surrounding sections are also tactile, the reader may hit visual fatigue by the time they arrive. Works best when the sections around it are quieter, like after a calm manifesto and before a spare testimonial rail.

What this asks the reader

Pick any card first. Read them in any order. You will still end up with the same picture of me.

How to choose

Which shape holds Yanik best.

All three options answer the brief. They differ in how much of the reader they ask to show up with, how much of Yanik's voice they use, and how much execution risk they carry.

OptionConf.Feels likeBest whenTrades off
01 · The handoff 89 A private letter slid across a table. You want the warmest, most emotionally generous answer, and a reader willing to slow down and read one page of prose. Requires a few real handwritten phrases scanned from Yanik, not a Caveat swap. Some readers will scroll past long form on a landing page.
02 · The mirror 92 Finishing someone else's sentence. You want the highest conversion lift and the tightest copy craft. Readers self select in by line three. Demands parallel copy that actually rhymes. Mediocre copy collapses the effect. Requires the single right portrait.
03 · The confession 82 A desk after midnight, cards laid out. You want the most shareable, most screenshot ready, most Instagrammable section, and a reader who skims. Visually busy. Fights surrounding sections for attention if they are also tactile.
My recommendation

Build Option 02, The Mirror. It is the strongest conversion play on the page, the clearest use of Yanik's voice, and the cleanest design solution. Then plant Option 01's letter as Section 08, a closing letter from him to the reader, so the mirror is the handshake and the letter is the farewell. Index cards (Option 03) we hold for a smaller section later, probably the About footer strip, where their density will not fight anything.
